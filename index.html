Ministry of Equal Distinctions · Introduction

Introduction

Scroll to read ↓

In recent weeks, we have looked beyond race alone, moving across continents, cultures, and histories to trace the wider architectures of human stratification. Through caste, colour, hierarchy, systemic oppression, untouchability, and indigeneity, we have sought to assemble a conceptual framework for the twenty-first century: one capable not only of naming historical injustice, but of imagining global solidarities, insisting on genuine representation, and demanding reparative justice for the marginalised.

Yet if these hierarchies are to be dismantled, their origins must first be understood. It is tempting to locate the beginnings of racial categorisation in the intellectual errors of the Enlightenment: in Johann Friedrich Blumenbach's 1775 taxonomy of mankind, which divided the human species into five principal categories or "ideal types" — Caucasians, Mongolians, Ethiopians, Americans, and Malays — or in the 1741 Bordeaux Academy contest, which explained Blackness through biblical curses and climate. These moments present themselves as convenient points of departure, as though racism were born simply from mistaken ideas.

But to stop there would be to misunderstand the problem. Scientific inquiry may begin as a search for truth, yet the knowledge it produces is never formed in innocence, for truth itself is so often shaped by what power permits, defines, and preserves. Under unequal relations of power, knowledge rarely remains pure; it is turned into an instrument that names, orders, and rules.

Categories that appear merely descriptive or explanatory can become technologies of domination once embedded in law, commerce, empire, education, museums, and scholarship. In a deeply Foucauldian sense, knowledge is never innocent: it is shaped by power even as it serves to reinforce it.

The scientific exploration of human variation was thus converted into one of the great engines of racial oppression. What began as classification became justification: a means of legitimising the exploitation of cheap labour, of dividing and weakening the working class, of securing prestige for elites, and of soothing the moral disquiet that domination might otherwise provoke. In this way, arbitrary hierarchies were recast as objective truths, and domination clothed itself in the language of reason.

At the heart of these many forms of oppression lies, I would argue, a deeper sustaining mechanism: epistemological oppression. Its violence exceeds the physical and the psychological; it reaches into the very conditions of thought itself, shaping how people understand the world, how they inhabit it, and how they come to know themselves. If reality is never simply given but carefully arranged, then epistemological oppression preserves structures of differentiation through the power to determine what can be known, whose knowledge counts, and how the world is made intelligible in the first place.

It operates first through the silencing of subaltern voices. As Forbes suggests, elite-centred scholarship repeatedly compels history to be seen through a white or otherwise privileged lens. As Professor Matache explains in relation to Roma communities, marginalised peoples are denied the means to narrate their own cultures and are instead made legible only through the categories of a dominant and oppressive order. Mohabir similarly shows how Western academic paradigms have long dismissed aural cultures and oral histories as illegitimate, excluding entire modes of knowing from the realm of the credible.

From this emerges a second dynamic: when the story of society is told only from above, the assumptions of elites become naturalised as unquestionable common sense. What is historically constructed in the service of power begins to appear natural, and contingency puts on the mask of inevitability. In the context of the Indian caste system, Berreman and Dr Ambedkar both observe that elites preserve their supremacy by cloaking status in elaborate philosophical, religious, and pseudo-genetic explanations, disguising exclusion as moral order and social necessity.

When reality is constructed in this way, hierarchy and exclusion are sustained not merely by coercion, but by consent. Neary notes that the historical Japanese state built a "structure of consent" around caste discrimination so pervasive that its origins became inseparable from local prejudice itself. Ultimately, then, as Professors Matache and West argue, so long as white normativity prevails, the supremacy of the dominant remains lodged at the centre of global dogma, often compelling the oppressed into compliance simply in order to survive.

End of Introduction

Context

While this project is shaped by and deeply values the many efforts to challenge hierarchy through advocacy, protest, and collective action, it begins from a different, though related, point of departure. Across the world, ordinary people are refusing silence, asserting their own voices, and animating movements of profound political significance. My intervention is situated within this broader context, but it turns to an epistemological question: if domination is sustained not only by violence and exclusion, but also by structuring the conditions of knowledge, then we must ask how knowledge itself is organised, authorised, and weaponised in the present.

This question feels especially urgent in a moment of intense political pressure, where education is increasingly contested and instrumentalised. The issue is not only what we know, but how we come to know at all. If hierarchy survives by making itself appear natural, then one task of critique is to unsettle the very conditions under which that appearance becomes believable.

The Project

It is for this reason that the project takes the form of a fictional museum exhibition. Set in the year 2078, in Harmony City, in the country known as the United States of Perfect Balance, the exhibition is organised by the Ministry of Equal Distinctions in collaboration with the Institute for Keeping Things in Order. Even before the visitor enters, the language of the institution signals the central irony of the project: this is a world in which inequality has not disappeared, but has instead been absorbed into the rhetoric of harmony, balance, and administrative reason.

The exhibition opens with the discovery of a fictional travel text, The Stranger's Atlas: An Annotated Survey of Peoples, Customs, and Social Boundaries, written by the fictional traveller Alistair Finch Hatton during his journeys across the late nineteenth- and early twentieth-century world. From there, visitors move through five galleries: devoted to Japan and East Asia, Europe, Africa, and the Americas and Caribbean. Across these spaces, the exhibition draws on historical literature to present observations about peoples, customs, and social boundaries as they might appear through the eyes of an outsider.

This narrative choice is deliberate. Because the exhibition is mediated through Finch Hatton's perspective, systems of caste, racialisation, social exclusion, and hierarchy are described as though they are simply there to be observed — narrated in the tone of detached record, as if they were natural features of social life rather than the contingent and violent products of history. The project seeks to expose how structures of domination so often become intelligible only through the perspective of those who classify, govern, and record, while the voices of those who live under these arrangements are marginalised or erased.

The Museum as Form

The museum is especially useful because it stands at the intersection of knowledge production, preservation, and authority. Through categorisation, curation, arrangement, and display, museums actively construct what counts as history, what counts as evidence, and what counts as legitimate understanding. From a Foucauldian perspective, museum curation does not merely present established knowledge; it helps produce and stabilise relations of power. As Greenhill (1992) and Bennett (2009) have shown, museums are not only pedagogical spaces, but institutions deeply involved in the regulation of conduct, the formation of public culture, and the organisation of modern social order.

That is why the exhibition is written in a tone reminiscent of Orwellian dystopia — exaggerated, bureaucratic, and unsettlingly calm. In the tradition of speculative fiction, what Suvin (2016) describes through the logic of cognitive estrangement, the exhibition constructs an alternative reality that remains legibly connected to our own. By encountering a world that feels both unfamiliar and disturbingly plausible, the audience is invited to recognise the structures that ordinary language so often conceals. Ultimately, The Exhibition as an Epistemological Critique asks not only what race is, but who gets to define these classifications in the first place — and whose worlds, histories, and humanity are rendered intelligible in the process.

Epistemological Critique

Welcome, then, to The Exhibition as an Epistemological Critique: a museum of order, hierarchy, and human difference, where the categories that once claimed merely to describe the world are revealed as instruments that helped to make it. What awaits is not a neutral archive of the past, but an encounter with the epistemic structures through which domination has been rendered sensible, legitimate, and enduring. If this project has a horizon, it is not simply exposure, but interruption: the unsettling of those conditions of knowledge that continue to determine whose histories are preserved, whose voices are authorised, and whose humanity is made intelligible.

On the occasion of this exhibition

The Ministry of Equal Distinctions is pleased to present this exhibition in collaboration with the Institute for Keeping Things in Order. At its center is a recently donated volume, The Stranger's Atlas: An Annotated Survey of Peoples, Customs, and Social Boundaries, preserved in exceptional condition and recognized as an important example of early classificatory practice.

Compiled by the traveler Alistair Finch Hatton, the volume brings together field notes, illustrated postcards, and related materials collected over the course of his journeys. These documents reflect a consistent effort to observe, record, and organize human difference.

That effort now appears especially significant.

It is sometimes argued that equality requires the removal of distinctions. This exhibition proceeds from a different understanding. Equality depends not on sameness, but on the accurate recognition of difference. Distinctions must not be denied or confused. They must be identified, maintained, and correctly understood.

Order depends upon this.

A society that cannot distinguish clearly cannot function fairly. Without classification, there can be no clarity. Without clarity, no stable form of harmony can be achieved.

The materials presented here demonstrate an early commitment to these principles. What may once have been regarded as neutral observation can now be understood as part of a broader administrative logic: the proper arrangement of social difference in the interest of collective balance.

From this perspective, equality is not the erasure of boundaries, but their correct expression.

This exhibition invites viewers to consider not whether distinctions exist, but how they are recognized, how they are managed, and how they are preserved.

Japan: Order and Its Administration

Scroll to read

↓

In Japan, order was not imagined. It was administered. Harmony depended upon the exact placement of every person within a visible and enduring structure of role, duty, and rank. Each life was assigned its proper relation to the whole, and through that arrangement society appeared to function with remarkable calm. Modern Japan is often praised for its efficiency, discipline, and social coherence. Such stability did not emerge from abstraction. It was achieved through the careful maintenance of equal distinctions.

Within this system, communities later identified as Burakumin were assigned to the management of death, blood, animal remains, and other forms of matter held to threaten the purity of ordinary life. These were not peripheral tasks. They were indispensable. By containing what the mainstream population refused to touch, they preserved the cleanliness, comfort, and moral order upon which the larger social body depended.

For the arrangement to hold, distinctions had to remain legible. Difference could not be left uncertain. It had to be marked, inherited, and immediately recognized. Occupation, residence, lineage, and appearance were aligned so that status announced itself before speech was necessary. In 1781 Hiroshima, for example, kawata were required to adopt specific hairstyles in order to make them instantly identifiable to the general population. They were also prohibited from wearing common clothing and forbidden from smoking or drinking with non-outcastes. Boundaries were not merely observed. They were displayed.

Travelers and officials described this condition as orderly, even admirable: a society in which every element knew its place and every impurity was correctly assigned. What they recorded as balance was sustained by exclusion made ordinary, by hierarchy made visible, and by a discipline so complete it could be mistaken for harmony itself.

Comparable Dynamics in East Asia

Comparable dynamics have sometimes been identified elsewhere in East Asia. Some scholars have pointed, cautiously, to the Liangshan region of Sichuan, where the Yi maintained a strongly ranked social order distinguishing the high-status nuoho, or "Black Yi," from the lower-status quho, or "White Yi." In some accounts, the quho, including populations described historically as dependent or enslaved, were treated as polluted, and marriage boundaries were strictly enforced. Professor Takezawa has noted that such distinctions were understood by some local actors as legible even in outward appearance. Yet the historical record here remains incomplete, and the comparison should not be made too neatly. However suggestive the parallel may appear, the history is more complicated than any single formula allows.

At least three broad ways of explaining this history can be identified. The first is a genetic-origin discourse. In this view, the Liangshan hierarchy is compared to the Indian caste system and is sometimes explained through claims of South Asian ancestry among certain Yi groups, especially Black Yi. This argument often draws on fragments of genetic discussion, migration speculation, and expansive historical conjecture. Its conclusions are often dramatic. Its evidentiary basis is considerably less stable.

The second is a more official historical discourse. This account places the Yi firmly within the long history of southwest China, with links to ancient Qiang populations and to social development across Yunnan, Sichuan, Guizhou, and neighboring regions. It acknowledges that Liangshan sustained a strict and enduring hierarchy, including Black Yi, White Yi, and other lower-status categories, but interprets this not as a foreign importation, but as a local historical formation: a regional system of rank, labor, kinship, and power that developed over time within China itself.

The third is a social-conflict discourse. Here the question of origin recedes, and the emphasis falls instead upon domination. In this framing, Black Yi appear as a ruling minority who preserved authority through blood hierarchy, marriage restriction, inherited rank, and the degradation of White Yi and, at times, Han populations. The system is described less as a cultural inheritance than as an instrument of control.

A fourth position, emerging partly in response to public misunderstanding, accepts that Black Yi and White Yi were historically distinct status groups in Liangshan, but resists the flattening simplifications that often follow. The distinction was shaped through overlapping differences of rank, economy, marriage, ritual practice, custom, and local history.

Taken together, these debates show the limits of any easy comparison. Similar dynamics may be observed, but resemblance is not identity. The language of purity, rank, exclusion, and inherited difference travels easily across cases; history does not. What remains clear is that systems of distinction were made durable not only through law or force, but through repetition, visibility, and social memory.
